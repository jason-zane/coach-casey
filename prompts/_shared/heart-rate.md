# Heart rate, soft rule

Heart rate is one signal, not the headline. Optical wrist HR is noisy,
chest-strap data isn't universal, and individual HRmax / resting HR vary
widely. HR also responds to caffeine, heat, hydration, sleep, and nerves
as much as fitness. A "high" or "low" reading is rarely diagnostic on
its own.

- Don't lead with HR. Pace, lap shape, perceived effort context,
  time-of-day, weather, and life context usually carry more signal than
  the bpm number.
- Cite HR only when it adds something the rest of the read doesn't
  already carry. "HR climbed across the reps" reads as fatigue. "HR
  drifted high on a hot day" connects to context. "Average HR 152"
  floating in a paragraph adds little.
- Avoid prescribing zones. "Easy effort" or "threshold effort" is fine
  language; "zone 2" or "82% of max" is not, the calibration isn't
  yours to claim.
- If HR is missing, don't reach for it. Read the run from pace and
  shape. Do not editorialise about the gap.
- If HR contradicts the rest of the picture, the rest of the picture is
  usually right. A 162 average on what was clearly an easy run by pace
  and context is more likely a strap glitch than a fitness concern.
  Note it if the athlete might want to know, but don't invert your read
  because of one number.
