# Coach Casey — Strategy Foundation

**Last updated:** 2026-04-23

## 1. Thesis

**Coach Casey is the interpretive layer for runners following a marathon plan.**

You've got a plan — from a book, a coach, an app, wherever. Coach Casey is the voice that's there every time you finish a run, that knows your history and your life, and that helps you make sense of what just happened. Not prescriptive. Not your coach. The reflective partner that fills the gap between finishing the run and having the next conversation with the person who wrote your plan.

**Tagline:** *Doesn't write your plan. Helps you run it.*

**What this means operationally:**
- All interactions are retrospective — interpretation, reflection, debrief
- Never prescriptive — no plan generation, no session instructions, no "do X tomorrow"
- Always available, but not real-time in the during-the-run sense
- Supplementary to whatever plan and/or coach the athlete already has

**What this rules out (deliberately):**
- Plan generation and programme writing — belongs to the coach or the book, not Coach Casey
- In-run coaching or real-time cueing during a workout
- Direct medical, nutrition, or injury advice that crosses into clinical territory
- Competing with the athlete's existing coach or plan on their turf

## 2. Positioning

**Supplementary, not substitutive.** This is the defensive position *and* the liability posture. Coach Casey sits next to the coach/plan, not in its place. Preserving this boundary is what keeps the product defensible as coaches adopt it, what keeps it out of the prescriptive-advice liability zone, and what differentiates it from the Runna/TrainingPeaks layer.

**Retrospective-first.** The interpretive, reflective, looking-back stance is the whole product. Every surface — chat, post-run debriefs, weekly reviews — operates in this mode.

**Anchored to coaching, not apps.** The market reflex is to compare Coach Casey to Runna, Strava Premium, TrainingPeaks. Reject this framing in marketing and onboarding copy. Position against the human coaching tier — "the reflective partner you don't currently have, for a fraction of a coach's cost." This makes $24/month feel cheap next to $150+ coaching, rather than expensive next to $20 apps.

### Brand architecture

Coach Casey is a **standalone product brand** with its own domain (coachcasey.app), its own identity, and its own marketing surface. It is not branded as "Coach Casey by The Marathon Clinic" — the clinic is not a parent brand in the user-facing sense.

**The Marathon Clinic is a separate property** (themarathonclinic.com): a content and philosophy hub where Jason's coaching thinking lives publicly. See §9 for the phased buildout. Attribution exists in the founder story ("Built by Jason, founder of The Marathon Clinic"), not in the product name or user-facing brand.

**Why this architecture:**

- **Keeps Coach Casey brand-neutral for the deferred B2B coach tier.** Coaches reselling Coach Casey to their own athletes do not need to reconcile the product with a competing-branded philosophy hub. "Configure Coach Casey with your philosophy" is a cleaner pitch than "use a product branded under another coach's clinic."
- **Preserves Coach Casey's optionality.** A standalone product brand can scale, pivot, or be acquired independently. A "by The Marathon Clinic" product is architecturally coupled to Jason's personal brand in ways that limit long-term paths.
- **Gives The Marathon Clinic a real job.** Rather than being a credibility wrapper without an operating function, it becomes the place where Jason-the-coach exists publicly — long-form content, coaching philosophy, the articulation of the voice Coach Casey embodies at scale. Same shape as Jay Johnson's authority hub at coachjayjohnson.com, which flows products from its underlying authority.
- **Removes architectural complexity from the launch surface.** Single-brand products are easier to market, explain, and remember. The two-brand framing was quietly adding cognitive load to every piece of Coach Casey messaging.

## 3. ICP

**Primary ICP:** Plan-following marathon runners without a regular interpretive voice beside them.

The common thread is *has a plan, has no reflective partner*. This explicitly includes:
- Book-plan runners (Pfitzinger, Hansons, Daniels, Hal Higdon)
- App-plan runners (Runna, TrainingPeaks self-serve, Garmin coach)
- Light-touch individual coaching clients (coach writes a plan, minimal weekly follow-up)
- Group-coaching clients where the coach can't give 1:1 attention

**Performance band:** marathon goal time 2:45–3:45. Marketing skews toward the competitive 2:45–3:15 range (aspirational hook); product volume sits in the 3:00–3:45 committed tier. Both bands are served by the same product.

**Geography:** AU/NZ as initial distribution focus — founder presence and the AU/NZ running community where Jason has credibility. The Marathon Clinic content platform (see §9) is the long-term authority anchor in the same region. Explicit intent to broaden later; this is a concentration for launch, not a permanent geographic bet.

**Behavioural signals:** Strava-native, training toward a goal race with some structural progression, reflective enough to value retrospective feedback (not just a plan-and-numbers runner).

**Frequency is a noisy proxy.** 4x/week on Pfitz 18/55 is in. 6x/week fun-running with no race goal is out. The question is *plan + purpose*, not volume.

## 4. Anti-ICP

Who this is explicitly not for:

- **Elite runners in deeply integrated coach-athlete relationships.** No gap for Coach Casey to fill; the coach already provides the interpretive layer.
- **Runners without a goal-driven plan.** Nothing structural to interpret against. Retrospective analysis is noise without a purpose to measure against.
- **Casual runners without a progression or race they're building toward.** Too little signal in the data to interpret meaningfully; product value is thin.

Naming anti-ICP matters because the ICP widened when we shifted from "coached runners" to "plan-following runners." Without explicit exclusions, "broader" drifts into "blurry."

## 5. The moat — the hidden product

**The compounding life-aware athlete memory is the real moat.**

Not the AI. Not the Strava integration. Not the retrospective framing. What makes Coach Casey structurally hard to leave after six months of use is the accumulated, longitudinal, life-aware memory of a specific athlete: their activities, their PRs, their injuries and niggles, their work stress, the sick kid, the pregnancy, the calf twinge from February, the goal race and why it matters.

**Why this matters strategically:**

- **Strava has the activity data.** Strava could plausibly ship "ask your activity" next quarter and match Coach Casey on the data axis.
- **Strava structurally will not integrate life context.** Work stress, sleep, family, injuries, emotional relationship with a goal race — none of this is in their data model, and none of it fits their product purpose.
- Coach Casey's defensibility lives in the integration of training data *with* life context, accumulated over time, per-athlete.

**This has implications for product, positioning, and retention:**

- It should be the **headline of the thesis**, not a sub-point — "knows you and your life" is the main claim.
- It determines the **retention window** that matters: the moat takes roughly 3 months to start being felt, 6 months to be compelling, 12 months to be structurally sticky.
- It justifies **annual pricing bias** (see §7): the compounding window is exactly the period you want someone committed through.
- It identifies the **signal worth measuring**: life context references in chat, qualitative "knows me better" reports, retention past month 3.

## 6. Competitive positioning

**Not competing (different product layer):**
- Runna, TrainingPeaks, Final Surge — plan-generation and plan-delivery. Coach Casey runs on top of their output.
- Coaches — complementary by design. Coach Casey is not a coach replacement.

**The real threat (18-month horizon):**
- Strava's own AI features. If Strava ships "ask about your activity" natively, the data-layer advantage disappears.
- Garmin Connect, WHOOP, Oura doing similar analysis within their ecosystems.

**Defensibility answer:**
- Life context integration (see §5). Strava won't build a system that knows you were up three times last night with a sick toddler, or that your goal race is the first marathon since your partner's diagnosis, or that you're trying to prove something to yourself after an injury layoff.
- The longitudinal, per-athlete accumulation is the moat. Generic LLM analysis of a single activity is table-stakes; Coach Casey's answer is always shaped by everything it knows about you across time and life.

## 7. Pricing

**Hypothesis (launch):**
- **Monthly: AU$24**
- **Annual: AU$199** (effective ~AU$16.60/month)
- **No free tier.** 7–14 day trial only.

**Reasoning:**

*Why not the app tier ($10–20).* Sits Coach Casey in the wrong mental bucket. Attracts casual users who won't engage with retrospective depth, who'll churn fast, who won't validate the thesis. Fights apps on apps' turf.

*Why not the high end ($30+).* Launching with no reputation, no testimonials, no social proof. Friction at this stage costs more than it will at V3. Pricing up later is a known-good move once you have case studies; pricing down signals something's broken. Give yourself headroom.

*Why $24 specifically.* Above the app ceiling (Runna and TrainingPeaks at ~$20 AU) so you're not in the app bucket. Under $25 psychologically. Makes the stacking math land better (Strava + Runna + Coach Casey = ~$59, not $65).

*Why the annual is the real strategic move.* The moat compounds over roughly 3–6 months. A monthly subscriber might churn at month 2, before the "knows me" effect kicks in. An annual subscriber stays through the compounding window by default. The discount isn't money given away — it's LTV recouped through retention.

*Why no free tier.* Dilutes positioning, attracts the wrong ICP, undercompensates for LLM inference costs. A trial is sufficient to handle the "try before buy" need.

**This is a hypothesis, not a commitment.** Test higher with later cohorts if conversion is strong; test lower if it's weak. Launch pricing is a variable.

**Anchor in marketing:** less than one coaching session per month, every month. Makes $24 feel cheap against $150+ coaching, rather than expensive against $20 apps.

## 8. Success criteria

"100–200 users" is a count, not a validation. Real criteria below.

### Primary — does the thesis hold?

- **Month 3 retention ≥ 65%.** Tests value past the initial-curiosity window.
- **Month 6 retention ≥ 50%.** Tests whether the moat is compounding. **The single most important number** — if this drops sharply, the "gets better with time" thesis is broken.
- **Month 12 retention ≥ 35%.** Tests whether Coach Casey is structurally hard to leave at the point where the moat should be fully formed.
- **Qualitative signal: ≥ 60% of month-3 users affirm Coach Casey is "knowing them better" than at month 1.** Via in-app prompt or NPS-adjacent survey. The thesis's own self-report.

### Secondary — is the product used the way it's designed to be?

- **Weekly active users ≥ 70%** of paying subscribers (at least one meaningful interaction per week — debrief, chat, or weekly review).
- **Engagement with debriefs and weekly reviews specifically**, not just chat. If chat is loved but the retrospective surfaces are ignored, the thesis is off.
- **Life context references in chat** by month 2 (work, stress, injuries, life events). If this isn't happening, the life-aware-memory moat isn't forming.

### Commercial

- 100–200 paying users within 6 months of launch.
- Monthly churn ≤ 8%.
- CAC:LTV > 1:3.

### Anti-signals worth watching explicitly

- Users churning specifically in month 2–3 ("it's nice, but I'm done")
- High chat engagement with low debrief engagement (novelty, not thesis)
- Power users who stop referencing life context after month 1 (moat isn't building)
- Monthly subscribers churning before month 3 at disproportionate rates (see §9 — annual conversion is a lever here)

## 9. Open questions and deferred decisions

### Open — V1-launch-adjacent

- **Annual conversion mechanics.** The moat compounds over 3–6 months, which is exactly the window where a monthly subscriber is most likely to churn — they haven't felt the "knows me" effect yet, the novelty has worn off, and they're asking "is this still worth $24?" The retention problem isn't solved by a better monthly product alone; it's solved by getting people onto annual before month 3, or giving monthly users a reason to stay past it.

  Four mechanics worth designing deliberately during V1 launch-prep:

  1. **Onboarding framing.** *"Most runners see the biggest benefit from month 3. Start with annual to get the full effect."* Thesis-aligned, honest, not a hard sell. Simplest to ship, lowest risk. Works because it tells the truth about how the product compounds. **Default: in.**

  2. **Memory-as-progress.** Show the accumulated athlete context explicitly — *"Coach Casey knows 47 of your runs, 3 goal races, your 2025 injury story."* A visible meter for the moat. Creates loss-aversion at the point someone considers cancelling, because the thing they'd lose is tangible, not abstract. Requires product surface work beyond a pricing choice — a small persistent UI element, updated as context accumulates. **Default: probably in, requires design.**

  3. **Month-2 upsell trigger.** A well-timed nudge to convert from monthly to annual right when the value is starting to land but before churn risk peaks. Timing is everything: too early feels pushy, too late loses the window. Probably delivered via the weekly review — the surface that's already earning its place in the athlete's attention. Should be a genuine offer ("lock in annual now, we'll credit the month you've paid"), not a dark pattern. **Default: in, design the specifics at launch-prep.**

  4. **Annual-exclusive features.** Risky — splitting the product dilutes the main thesis, and tier-gating features that belong in the core product is bad positioning. Only defensible if there's a feature that's *genuinely annual-shaped* — seasonal plan review, goal-race debrief, year-end training summary. **Default: out, unless a naturally annual-cadence feature emerges.** Don't manufacture one for the sake of the pricing tier.

  **Not a V1 day-one decision, but V1 launch-prep.** Worth solving deliberately, not defaulting to "monthly + annual checkbox at signup."

- **Trial length and shape.** 7 days vs 14 days. Free-trial vs money-back first month. Not yet decided. Bias: short enough to force conversion, long enough for the first weekly review to land.

### Open — brand architecture adjacent

- **The Marathon Clinic content platform (themarathonclinic.com).** A separate brand property positioned as Jason's coaching philosophy and thinking hub — the public-facing articulation of the voice that Coach Casey embodies. Serves three jobs: (a) authority anchor for AU/NZ launch credibility, (b) long-term SEO and top-of-funnel asset, (c) brand separation layer that keeps Coach Casey neutral for the B2B coach tier.

  **Phased buildout (deliberately slow):**

  1. **At V1 launch:** one-page placeholder at themarathonclinic.com naming the intent and linking to Coach Casey. Reserves the brand, signals the direction, no content operation yet.
  2. **Post-V1 (once Coach Casey has real users):** begin publishing at 1–2 posts per month. Topics drawn from actual Coach Casey user patterns. Sharp interpretive/philosophical angle — how to *think about* your training — not generic training advice. Voice: Jason's, distinct from Coach Casey's product voice.
  3. **6+ months post-launch:** assess whether the content is earning its keep (traffic, signup contribution, authority signals). Scale, maintain, or reconsider.

  **The discipline that matters most:** don't build Phase 2 in parallel with V1 build. Content operations silently draw attention from thesis-proving work. Phase 1 is a placeholder, nothing more, until V1 ships.

  **What would change it:** content platform becomes load-bearing for acquisition in a way we don't currently plan for (e.g., organic search becomes a primary signup channel) — then it stops being "deferred" and becomes an active workstream.

### Deferred — with triggers

- **B2B coach tier.** Coaches configure their philosophy and resell Coach Casey access to their athletes. Compelling long-game but explicitly not V1. The brand architecture revision (§2) materially improves this path — Coach Casey's standalone identity means coaches are not reselling a clinic-branded product. Trigger for revisit: post-launch validation of core product + unprompted interest from coaches.

- **Geographic expansion beyond AU/NZ.** Deliberate concentration for launch. Trigger: AU/NZ traction proven, or a clear opportunity (e.g. partnership, event, distribution) in an adjacent market.

### Out of scope for strategy (belongs elsewhere)

- **Acquisition and growth strategy.** Channels, messaging, funnel, partnerships. Follows once strategy + V1 scope are locked. Separate workstream.
- **Brand voice and tone.** Belongs in the content layer, informed by the thesis here.
- **Onboarding flow design.** Belongs in product-design, informed by the ICP and moat here.
